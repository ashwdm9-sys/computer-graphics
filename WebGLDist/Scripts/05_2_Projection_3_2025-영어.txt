Arcball is a software-implemented interface that allows you to control rotation
using just the mouse
This is a simulation of a real trackball device
A trackball device looks like the picture
and allows you to input rotation data by rolling a protruding ball with your hand
Now let's place the virtual hemisphere on the computer screen
A point (x, y, z) on the hemisphere can be mapped to a point (x, y, 0) on the screen
Conversely, let's assume that we have designated a point (x, y, 0) on the screen with the mouse
From this point, we can map points (x, y, z) on the opposite hemisphere
The x, y values are the same as the coordinates on the screen
Since the equation of a sphere is x^2
+ y^2 + z^2 = 1, the z value can be obtained as sqrt(1 - x^2 - y^2)
In other words, any location specified on the screen with the mouse can be mapped
to a 3D point coordinate on the arcball
So, how can we map the coordinates of the corners of the screen,
other than the part projected on the screen, i.e
the part marked in red in the picture, to a hemisphere?
The mouse coordinates in this section
are mapped to the nearest point on the hemisphere
Now, let's consider two points on the hemisphere,
p1 and p2, mapped from two mouse coordinates on the screen
Our goal is to find the rotation transformation
matrix that represents the input behavior when the mouse is dragged from p1 to p2
To obtain such a transformation,
we need to obtain the rotation axis and rotation angle
The rotation axis can be easily obtained by taking the cross product of two vectors
pointing from the origin to points p1 and p2 on the hemisphere
Rotation Angle can be obtained from the dot product of vectors p1 and p2
The dot product of p1 and p2 is, by definition,
the product of the magnitudes of p1 and p2 times cosθ
Here, θ is the angle between the two vectors p1 and p2
The two vectors p1 and p2 lie on the unit sphere with radius 1,
and the magnitude of both vectors is 1
Therefore, θ is equal to the arccos of the dot product of p1 and p2
Now, using the rotation axis and angle,
we can obtain the RotationTransformation matrix
using the rotation or Quaternion
formulas about any axis that we learned before
