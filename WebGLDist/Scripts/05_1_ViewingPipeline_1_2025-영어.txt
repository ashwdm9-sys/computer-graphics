
We have been using NDC, or Normalized Device
Coordinates, so far
In NDC, the range of each coordinate in the XYZ direction
is limited to -1 to 1
Objects that are not within this range will become invisible
However, these limitations are too unrealistic
From now on, we will be free to decide how much space we will use
And in Vertex Shader, this arbitrary space is transformed into NDC and the result
gl_Position is sent to the hardware
As you can see in the picture, a green rectangle
and a yellow quadrant are drawn on the camera boundary of a User Defined Space
with a size of 90 x 90
The Vertex Shader converts this to NDC between -1 and 1
and then sends it to the hardware
Vertex Coordinates are converted in several steps
Coordinates defined in local space are transformed to world space
by applying model transformation
and to view space by applying view transformation
Additionally, it is converted to clip space through projection transformation,
and then finally converted to screen space
using viewport transformation to be displayed on the screen
Local space, also called object space,
is a coordinate system limited to a specific object
For example, let's say
you're modeling a cube in graphics software like Blender
Usually, initially, the cube has a basic shape centered at the origin
and one edge has a length of 1
Afterwards, we resize this, move it,
or rearrange it
The coordinate system that an object initially uses to define itself
is called local space, and the coordinates of the vertices at this time
are called local coordinates
Objects defined in local space
are gathered in one space to form a scene
For example, if trees, cars, etc. all appear in one scene
Houses, trees, and cars would
all have been initially modeled in their own local spaces
For example, the center of each object is the origin
and the size will not be relatively adjusted
These objects must be placed together in one space,
and this one space is called World Space
World Coordinates refer to the coordinates defined within this World Space
As you can see in the figure,
the two rectangles that make up the robot's arm
are initially modeled as A and B in their respective local spaces
To move and place them in one World Space,
a transformation from local to world space is required
The matrix representing the transformation from local to world
is called the model matrix,
and this transformation is called the model transformation
In the figure, you can see that the cube is translated from local space
to world space according to the model transformation
The object in world space is transformed to view space
by the view transformation
View space is also called camera space or eye space
View space is the space defined around the user's view
As you can see in the picture, when objects exist in world space,
the view space is a space that has the Center Of Projection, or COP,
which is the location of the camera, as its origin
View Transformation, represented by the View
Matrix, transforms coordinates from World Space to View Space
The following is a transformation from View
Space to Clip Space
Clip Space is defined by Clip Coordinate, which is NDC
As I've mentioned several times,
the final output gl_Position in the Vertex Shader must have NDC coordinates
NDC means that all coordinates
of XYZ are in the range -1 to 1
Any coordinates that are not within this range will not be visible on the screen
For example, points like (-2, 0, 0), (0.2, 0.5, -5)
The transformation from view space to clip space
is done by the projection matrix
In particular, the projection transformation can be separated into two steps:
Projection and Perspective Division
The last remaining step is to transform the coordinates in clip
space to viewport coordinates
and convert them to screen space coordinates
Screen space is the pixel coordinates that appear on the actual display screen
In fact, this viewport
transformation happens automatically by the graphics hardware,
so we don't have to program it
