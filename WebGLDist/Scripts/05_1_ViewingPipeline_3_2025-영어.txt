Cameras play an important role in computer graphics
Even if the same objects are fixed in the 3D world,
the final output image will be different
depending on how you look at the objects
This is similar to how the camera
position and direction when taking a portrait
can result in different photos of the same person
The picture shows the game view and working view of the Unity game engine
The cameras used in these two views are different
Depending on where you place the camera (marked in white in the Working view)
and where you look, the scene you see in the actual game
in the upper game view will change
The direction of the white camera icon in the working view

As mentioned before, the camera in OpenGL
is always located at the Origin and facing the -Z direction
We cannot change this position and direction
So how do we move the camera to the desired position
and direction to get the view we want?
It simulates the motion of the camera
That is, it makes it feel like the camera is moving by moving the object
visible in the current view
in the opposite direction of the camera's motion
This is similar to when you make a train interior set for a movie
and move the trees outside the window to give the impression that the train is moving
In other words, the idea is to place objects in the opposite direction
instead of placing the camera
To define the camera's space,
various camera parameters are defined in world space
The World Coordinate System we are familiar with has X, Y, and Z axes as the basis,
which are perpendicular to each other, and is a right-handed coordinate system
That is, the positive direction of the Z-axis is pointing towards us from the screen
We define several important parameters to define the camera
P is the position of the camera, called Center of Projection, or COP for short
A represents the target point that the camera is looking at,
which we will call At Point here
V is the UP vector, which is a vector indicating the direction of the top of the camera's head as shown in the picture
Now, let's define the view, i.e
Camera (Eye) coordinate system using the parameters above
We will find the three axes of this coordinate system: U, V, and N
U is the axis vector in the right direction of the camera,
which corresponds to the X-axis in the general coordinate system
V is the axis vector indicating the upward direction of the camera, i.e
the Y direction, as defined earlier
Finally, N represents the axis in the direction the camera is looking,
which corresponds to the +Z axis in the general coordinate system
N vector is a vector going from P to A
Also, N is equal to the normal vector of the view plane
However, as you can see in the picture, the +N direction is opposite to the world space,
and the +N direction is pointing inward toward the screen from our side
In other words, the view coordinate system can be said to be a left-handed coordinate system
Based on this definition of UVN,
we will look at how to obtain the actual UVN in the next slide
Now let's look at how to compute the UVN axes
from the given camera parameters P, A, V
First, let's assume that instead of the up vector V, a virtual up
vector V' is given as a parameter
v' is an approximation vector that represents the approximate UP direction,
not the exact V direction vector
In fact, the vector going from P to A is N, and N and V must
be perpendicular to each other
However, it is not easy for users to directly calculate these vectors
and input them as camera parameters
So, we give a roughly similar v'
and let the program calculate the exact V
In many cases, the Y-axis (0, 1, 0) of world space is used as v'
Of course, if you're holding the camera upside down, the -Y axis might be better
Calculating N is the easiest
If the vector (A - P) from P to A is n,
then normalizing n gives N
The U vector is perpendicular to both V and N
We obtain u by taking the cross product of n and v'
and normalize it to obtain U
If you look closely, you can find something strange
Since it is a UVN coordinate system,
you might think that to find U, you should do V x N rather than N x V
But that's for the right-handed coordinate system
As we saw before, since UVN in camera space is a left-handed coordinate system,
we calculated U by calculating N x V, which is the opposite direction of V x N
Now that we have N and U, we can find the exact V
We can do u x n
to find v, and normalize this to get V
As above, since we compute the cross product
in the opposite direction to the
right-handed coordinate system, we compute u x n, not n x u
